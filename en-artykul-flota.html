Fleet Types & Technical Specifications

The following article provides a comprehensive overview of the transport fleet available on the market. You will understand the structural differences between vehicle types and learn how to properly match the equipment to the specifics of your cargo to optimize logistics costs.

1. Body Types

01. Curtainsider

The cornerstone of European road transport. The trailer is equipped with flexible, sliding side curtains made of reinforced PVC tarpaulin, maximizing the vehicle's utility.

Advantages:Extreme versatility. Allows simultaneous loading from both sides, the rear, and in most cases, through a sliding roof (for overhead cranes). It boasts the highest availability on the spot market.
Disadvantages:The curtain rail design reduces the side loading clearance by about 5–10 cm compared to the internal height. Additionally, the flexible tarpaulin is susceptible to theft attempts (so-called "tarpaulin slashing").
Standard Loading:Side, Rear, Top.

02. Standard Trailer (Drop-side)

A classic design that is slowly giving way to curtainsiders. The tarpaulin does not slide on rollers but is manually fastened to eyelets and a TIR cable around rigid aluminum sideboards.

Advantages:Very solid sideboard construction. Ensures excellent stabilization for loads that might exert lateral pressure on the walls (e.g., loose big-bags).
Disadvantages:Handling is highly labor-intensive. To load goods from the side, the driver must completely unfasten the tarpaulin, throw it over the roof, and dismantle the stanchions and sideboards, drastically extending loading times at warehouses.
Availability:High (especially popular on Eastern routes).

03. Mega Trailer

A specialized curtainsider designed to maximize cargo volume. Thanks to the use of "low-deck" tractor units (low fifth wheel, low-profile tires), the trailer floor is lowered.

Advantages:Impressive internal height reaching 3.00 meters, allowing for a volume of around 100 m³. Indispensable in the Automotive industry (allows stacking of gitterboxes on 3 levels) and in the transport of insulation materials and packaging. Often features a lifting roof for loading.
Disadvantages:Very low ground clearance makes the combination prone to damage on uneven terrain, construction sites, or bumpy parking lots.

04. Jumbo (Step-deck / Road Train)

A design utilizing a "step-deck" floor. The front section (above the kingpin) is higher, while the main cargo deck is dropped as low as possible.

Advantages:The lowered deck offers massive space and above-standard internal height (over 3 meters) without violating strict European limits on maximum external vehicle height (4.00 m). Ideal for tall agricultural machinery or tanks.
Disadvantages:The internal step prevents the loading of very long, rigid elements that would rest on the edge. Requires clever planning of pallet distribution.

05. Refrigerated Truck (Reefer)

A body with hard, thick, and thermally insulated walls, integrated with a professional cooling unit (e.g., Thermo King, Carrier).

Advantages:Allows rigorous temperature control of the cargo, from deep freezing (-25°C) to heating (+25°C). Essential for pharmaceuticals (GDP certificates) and fresh food. Rigid walls eliminate the risk of cargo slashing and theft. Additionally, in many European countries, food transports are exempt from weekend and holiday driving bans.
Disadvantages:The unit and thickened walls increase the vehicle's unladen weight (lower payload of around 22-22.5t instead of 24t). Due to insulation thickness, the internal width may be slightly narrower (usually 2.46m instead of 2.48m).
Loading:Rear only, via bumper and doors.

06. Box Trailer (Isotherm)

A closed, rigid trailer without an active cooling system. Serves exclusively to protect against external weather conditions.

Advantages:Armor-like cargo security. This is the preferred choice of tech companies (electronics, appliances transport) and the fashion industry (often fitted with garment rails for hanging clothes). Protection against moisture and intruders.
Disadvantages:Similar to a reefer, loading is only possible from the rear, which completely excludes handling long and heavy goods loaded by crane or from the side. High unladen weight limits maximum payload.

07. Tanker

Specialized cylindrical units used for bulk transport of liquids, eliminating the need for pallets or IBC tanks (Mausers).

Advantages:Unrivaled efficiency in volumetric transport of liquids such as fuels, industrial chemicals, or – in the case of specially certified food tankers – milk, liquid chocolate, or oils.
Disadvantages:Requires expensive certifications (often ADR), specific driver qualifications, and rigorous, documented chemical cleaning of the interior compartments after absolutely every transport performed. Low spot availability on freight exchanges.

08. Flatbed

A completely open trailer, devoid of walls, roof, or frame. A clean loading deck.

Advantages:No physical constraints resulting from the trailer's "box" skeleton. This is a strict requirement when transporting long steel profiles, concrete prefabricates, or industrial machines that may slightly protrude beyond the vehicle's outline. Usually equipped with heavy-duty securing sockets.
Disadvantages:The cargo is directly exposed to weather conditions (rain, road salt). Requires masterful lashing skills (cargo securing) using certified high-capacity chains and straps.

09. Lowbed

Trailers with an extremely lowered main deck, designed specifically for heavy engineering and oversized (OOG) cargo.

Advantages:Extremely low ground clearance (often 30-60 cm above the asphalt) and a lowered center of gravity allow for loading massive combine harvesters, crawler excavators, and transformers without fear of hitting bridges or overpasses. Equipped with their own heavy-duty hydraulic ramps.
Disadvantages:Operating such transports generates very high fixed costs. Due to cargo dimensions and trailer weight, these transports often fall into the OOG (Out of Gauge) category, requiring official permits, night driving, and escort (pilot) vehicles.

10. Dropside

A hybrid of a flatbed with a system of foldable, mostly aluminum sideboards around the deck.

Advantages:The sideboards physically prevent lateral cargo shifting, making this trailer ideal for transporting pallets of hollow blocks, PVC pipes, scaffolding elements, or loose material secured with a net.
Disadvantages:The additional weight of the sideboard system lowers the available payload. Moreover, the lack of a tarpaulin excludes moisture-sensitive cargo, unless manual tarping by the driver is applied, which is cumbersome.

11. Car Transporter

Specialized multi-level structure designed exclusively for vehicle transport.

Advantages:Thanks to a complex system of movable, hydraulic decks (ramps and traps), it can accommodate from a few SUVs to even 9-10 smaller passenger cars on a single run, optimizing distribution costs from factories to dealers.
Disadvantages:This is one-way and highly niche equipment. You cannot transport standard industrial pallets on a car transporter, meaning forwarders dealing in this market must operate in a very hermetic environment.

12. Tipper / Dumper

A "bathtub" style trailer integrated with a powerful telescopic cylinder lifting the front of the box, allowing for gravity unloading.

Application (Aluminum):Due to their low unladen weight, aluminum tippers dominate the volumetric transport of light and medium-heavy materials: grains, agricultural produce, biomass, or fertilizer granules.
Application (Steel):Much heavier, but exceptionally resistant to impacts and deformation. Essential on construction sites for transporting gravel, demolition rubble, coal, and hot asphalt.

13. Swap Body (BDF)

A system of modular cargo boxes. The truck (usually a rigid or tandem) can "drop" the cargo box directly onto its own support legs.

Advantages:The essence of logistics efficiency ("Drop & Swap"). The driver pulls up to a distribution center, detaches the full box (which will be slowly unloaded by warehouse staff), attaches an empty (or pre-loaded) box waiting nearby, and drives off in 15 minutes. A concept heavily utilized by courier companies and in intermodal transport (transfer to railway wagons).
Disadvantages:Requires a highly specialized, cohesive truck fleet and standardization among partners. Rarely found as a "free truck" on public freight exchanges.

14. Silo Trailer

A sealed, pressurized metal tank designed for transporting bulk materials, flours, and dusts.

Advantages:Protection against moisture (which could, for example, turn transported cement into rock). The biggest advantage is the unloading system: powerful compressors "blow" the goods from the trailer through pipes directly into vertical destination silos at factories or construction sites.
Disadvantages:Limited pool of carriers. Silos are difficult to clean thoroughly (risk of cross-contamination, e.g., flour after chemicals, which forces a strict division of the fleet into food-grade and technical).

15. Tele-trailer & Hooklift

Tele-trailer:A specialized flatbed equipped with an extendable telescopic beam. Its frame allows the structure to stretch lengthwise from a basic 13.6 m to even 30-40 meters. Essential for transporting wind turbine blades, steel bridge spans, or long industrial pipes. The operation requires perfect road logistics.
Hooklift:A truck chassis (not a trailer) fitted with a hydraulic arm ending in a hook. It pulls standardized roll-off containers (skips) onto its back. A system widely used in waste management, scrap transport, recycling, and construction rubble handling. The container exchange operation takes just a few minutes.

2. GVW Classification (Gross Vehicle Weight)

GVW (Gross Vehicle Weight) is the total weight of the vehicle including cargo, passengers, and operating fluids. In logistics, it determines not only how much goods we can load (Payload), but also what road regulations, toll fees (Maut/Viatoll), and urban restrictions apply to a given vehicle. The breakdown below presents market standards in a practical context:

Fleet Type GVW Category Approx. Payload Standard Capacity (Euro Pallets) Key Business Application
Vans (LCV - Light Commercial Vehicles) up to 3.5 t 0.6 t - 1.2 t 8, 10, or 12 EP Express Transport. Vehicles do not have digital tachographs (greater flexibility in driver working hours for dedicated transport) and are not subject to weekend and holiday truck driving bans. Ideal for rapid deliveries across Europe ("for yesterday"). They can freely enter city centers.
Small Rigid Trucks 3.5 t to 7.5 t 2.5 t - 3.0 t 10 - 15 EP "Last Mile" local distribution. A compromise solution when there are too many pallets for a van, but a classic heavy truck is too large and expensive for a given route. Due to lower weight, these vehicles can often bypass tonnage restrictions on lower category roads.
Medium Rigid Trucks 7.5 t - 12 t 5.0 t - 6.0 t 15 - 18 EP LTL (Less Than Truckload) regional distribution. The best operational balance between fuel/toll costs and space for arranging goods distributed between logistics hubs and larger wholesalers.
Large Rigid Trucks 12 t - 18 t 9.0 t - 10.0 t 18 - 22 EP High tonnage, low volumetric tonnage. Built for heavier loads, e.g., in construction, machinery, or beverage industry (water pallets), where the weight limit is reached before physical floor space runs out.

The most economically optimized form of transporting large batches of material over long distances on a pan-European scale. In some countries and in intermodal transport, regulations allow an increase of GVW to 44 tons.

3. Loading & Mechanical Systems

Knowledge of available technical enhancements allows forwarders to gain a massive competitive edge, mitigating the client's warehouse infrastructure shortages and streamlining the flow of goods. Here are the key systems:

Walking Floor:An innovative system of floor slats powered by hydraulics that alternately extend and retract, slowly inching the cargo forward or backward. Allows for complete unloading of bulk goods (sawdust, soil, waste) without the need for dangerous lifting of the trailer (like in tippers), enabling work inside low halls and on soft, uneven ground.
Joloda (Skate System):A system of metal channels (rails) built into the trailer floor and cooperating movable skates that gently lift the load when connected to compressed air or operated manually. The system is absolutely critical in the paper industry for loading massive, multi-ton rolls of newsprint, allowing them to be pushed deep and safely into the trailer.
Tail Lift:A fold-out, hydraulic platform mounted on the rear overhang of the truck. Relieves the shipper from the need to have a loading dock or a forklift. Goods (usually on Euro pallets) can be easily rolled onto the lift using a manual pallet jack and lowered to ground level. We distinguish classic lifts (cantilever – closing the rear of the vehicle) and tuck-away lifts (retractable underneath, invisible while driving).
Double Deck / Dopple-Stock:A double-level loading system found mainly in reefers. Using built-in wall tracks and crossbeams, a "second floor" is created in the trailer, independent of the main floor. This solution protects delicate and non-stackable goods from crushing and doubles the capacity from 33 to 66 Euro pallets on one truck, halving the carbon footprint per cargo unit.
