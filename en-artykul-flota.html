Fleet Types & Technical Specifications

The following article provides a comprehensive overview of the transport fleet available on the market. You will understand the structural differences between vehicle types and learn how to properly match the equipment to the specifics of your cargo to optimize logistics costs.

1. Body Types

01. Curtainsider

The cornerstone of European road transport. The trailer is equipped with flexible, sliding side curtains made of reinforced PVC tarpaulin.

Advantages:Extreme versatility. Allows simultaneous loading from both sides, the rear, and via a sliding roof. Highest spot market availability.
Disadvantages:Curtain design reduces side loading clearance by 5–10 cm. Flexible tarpaulin is susceptible to theft attempts ("tarpaulin slashing").

02. Standard Trailer (Drop-side)

A classic design slowly giving way to curtainsiders. The tarpaulin is manually fastened to eyelets and a TIR cable around rigid aluminum sideboards.

Advantages:Solid sideboard construction. Perfect stabilization for loads exerting lateral pressure on walls (e.g., loose big-bags).
Disadvantages:Labor-intensive side handling (completely unfasten tarpaulin, dismantle wooden slats and sideboards), extending loading windows.

03. Mega Trailer

A specialized curtainsider designed to maximize cargo volume through low-deck tractors (low fifth wheel, lower floor height).

Advantages:Internal height reaching 3.00 meters (volume around 100 m³). Critical for Automotive (stacking gitterboxes) and packaging transports.
Wady:Very low ground clearance makes the entire combination prone to damage on uneven terrain or bumpy sites.

04. Jumbo (Step-deck)

A design utilizing a step-deck floor. The front section is higher, while the main cargo deck behind the kingpin is dropped as low as possible.

Advantages:Lowered deck offers massive space and internal height over 3 meters without violating external height limits (4.00 m). Ideal for tall machinery.
Disadvantages:Internal step prevents loading very long, rigid elements resting on the edge. Requires smart pallet arrangement.

05. Refrigerated Truck (Reefer)

A closed body with hard, thick, and thermally insulated walls, integrated with an active cooling unit.

Advantages:Rigid temperature control (-25°C to +25°C). Critical for pharma (GDP) and fresh food. Rigid walls eliminate plandek cutting risk. Excluded from driving bans.
Disadvantages:Unit and walls lower available payload (to around 22-22.5t). Width might be slightly narrower (2.46m). Loading possible rear-only.

06. Box Trailer (Isotherm)

A closed, rigid trailer without active cooling. Protects exclusively against external weather conditions and security breaches.

Advantages:Maximum cargo protection. Preferred choice for electronics and the fashion industry (often fitted with hanging garment rails).
Disadvantages:Loading only possible from the rear, excluding side or crane handling. High unladen weight limits payload.

07. Tanker

Specialized cylindrical units used for bulk transport of liquids, avoiding pallets or IBC tanks.

Advantages:Unrivaled efficiency for liquid materials like fuels, chemicals, orcertified liquids (milk, liquid chocolate, oils).
Disadvantages:Requires expensive ADR certifications, driver training, and strict, documented chemical compartment cleaning after each run.

08. Flatbed

A completely open trailer, devoid of walls, roof, or frame. A clean loading deck with strong securing sockets.

Advantages:No physical constraints resulting from box skeletons. Crucial for long steel profiles, concrete prefabricates, or oversized machinery.
Disadvantages:Cargo directly exposed to rain and road salt. Requires heavy-duty lashing skills with high-capacity chains.

09. Lowbed

Trailers with an extremely lowered main deck, designed specifically for heavy engineering and oversized (OOG) cargo.

Advantages:Extremely low deck (30-60 cm) allows loading massive excavators or transformers without hitting bridges. Hydraulic ramps.
Disadvantages:Generates high operational costs. Often falls into the OOG category, requiring permits, night driving, and pilot escorts.

10. Dropside

A hybrid of a flatbed with a system of foldable aluminum sideboards around the deck.

Advantages:Sideboards block lateral cargo shifting, perfect for pallets of hollow blocks, pipes, or scaffolding elements.
Disadvantages:Sideboard system weight lowers the payload. Lack of tarp protection excludes moisture-sensitive cargo.

11. Car Transporter

Specialized multi-level structure designed exclusively for vehicle transport.

Advantages:Movable hydraulic decks accommodate up to 10 passenger cars on a single run, optimizing distribution costs from factories.
Disadvantages:Niche equipment ("one-way"). Cannot carry standard pallets, leading to higher empty-mile risk.

12. Tipper / Dumper

A bathtub trailer with a telescopic hydraulic cylinder lifting the front box for gravity unloading.

Advantages:Aluminum tippers dominate volumetric light cargo (grain, biomass). Steel variants are standard for construction (gravel, rubble, coal).
Disadvantages:Lifting excludes unloading inside low warehouses and requires completely flat terrain due to tipping risks.

13. Swap Body (BDF)

A modular cargo box system. The truck chassis can drop the cargo container on its own legs.

Advantages:KWintesencja wydajności ("Drop & Swap"). Driver swaps a full box for an empty one within 15 minutes. Heavy courier standard.
Disadvantages:Requires specialized truck configurations and partner cohesion. Rarely found as a free truck on exchanges.

14. Silo Trailer

A sealed, pressurized tank designed for transporting dusts, flours, and technical bulk powders.

Advantages:Compressors blow goods directly into destination silos. Absolute moisture protection prevents product clotting.
Disadvantages:Hermetic structure is hard to wash. Critical division forced between food-grade and technical units to avoid contamination.

15. Tele-trailer & Hooklift

Tele-trailer:Flatbed fitted with extendable beams, allowing extension up to 40 meters. Crucial for wind blades or long bridge structures.
Hooklift:Chassis fitted with a hydraulic arm pulling standardized roll-off containers. Heavily applied in recycling and rubble collection.

2. GVW Classification (Gross Vehicle Weight)

GVW determines payload capacities, toll structures (Maut), and urban restrictions:

Fleet Type GVW Category Approx. Payload Capacity (EP) Key Business Application
Vans (LCV) up to 3.5 t 0.6 t - 1.2 t 8 - 12 EP Express Transport. No tachographs or weekend truck bans. Rapid cross-border dedicated deliveries. Can enter city centers.
Small Rigid Trucks 3.5 t to 7.5 t 2.5 t - 3.0 t 10 - 15 EP Last Mile distribution. Balancing capacity when vans are too small. Often skips weight bans on smaller roads.
Medium Rigid Trucks 7.5 t - 12 t 5.0 t - 6.0 t 15 - 18 EP LTL regional distribution. Optimized balance between toll fees and cargo footprint for regional hubs.
Large Rigid Trucks 12 t - 18 t 9.0 t - 10.0 t 18 - 22 EP High tonaż, low volume. Built for construction, heavy components, or beverages where weight caps before floor space.
Heavy Rigid Trucks 18 t - 26 t up to 15.0 t 20 - 24 EP 3-axle rigid trucks, used for municipal, industrial, or heavy equipment movements inside urban districts.
Articulated Vehicles (FTL) 40 t 24.0 t - 25.0 t 33 - 38 EP Backbone of international logistics. Optimized long-haul transport across Europe. Intermodal expands limit to 44t.

3. Loading & Mechanical Systems

Walking Floor:Floor slats powered alternately by hydraulics. Allows unloading loose materials horizontally inside low warehouses without tipping risks.
Joloda (Skate System):Built-in floor channels and skates. Strictly required in the paper market to slide heavy newsprint rolls safely deep into the trailer body.
Tail Lift:Hydraulic platform opening at the rear. Allows the driver to lower cargo down to street level with a simple hand pallet jack, omitting forklifts.
Double Deck (Dopple-Stock):Two-level loading beam tracks used in reefers. Secures non-stackable pallets on two floors, expanding capacity to 66 pallets.

4. Additional Equipment

Lashing Straps with Ergo-Tensioner:Straps press cargo down to the floor. The ERGO variant features an elongated ratchet arm, easing heavy force application for steel or timber.
Anti-Slip Mats:Rubber sections maximizing floor friction coefficients, heavily reducing the number of mandatory lashing straps required.
Edge Protectors (Corners):Hard plastic or cardboard sections protecting boxes from crushing under strap tension while distributing forces evenly.
XL Certificate (EN 12642 XL):Structural body framework strength certificate. Tight form-fit loading permits walls to absorb kinetic forces, omitting manual strapping.
